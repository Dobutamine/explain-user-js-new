## Cover letter

Editor-in-Chief, *Pediatric Research*

Dear Dr. Bearer and Editors,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, **"An AI-assisted closed-loop method for patient-specific parameterization of a whole-body neonatal physiology model,"** for consideration as a **Basic Science Article** in *Pediatric Research*.

Mechanistic, lumped-parameter models of neonatal physiology are valued because their parameters are interpretable — each elastance, resistance or shunt geometry corresponds to something a clinician can reason about. But that same structure makes them very hard to individualize: a whole-body neonatal model has several hundred free parameters, while any given patient supplies only a handful of measured quantities — a heart rate, a blood pressure, a cardiac output or its surrogate, an oxygen saturation, a blood gas. Fitting the model to a patient is therefore a severely underdetermined inverse problem that has traditionally been solved by slow, irreproducible expert hand-tuning. This is not a peripheral inconvenience; it is the central reason mechanistic models remain largely at the margins of neonatal care, and it is why the recent in-silico neonatal models published in this journal are run with generic rather than patient-specific parameters — the most recent congenital-heart-disease transition model is evaluated explicitly "without patient-specific parameters." In this manuscript we describe the method by which EXPLAIN, the real-time whole-body neonatal simulator of our companion papers, is instead fitted to an individual patient automatically and reproducibly.

The method rests on separating two roles that manual tuning conflates. The part of the task that genuinely requires judgement — reading a messy clinical description and deciding what the targets and the pathophysiology are — is handled by an **interpretation layer**, a large language model, which translates the available information (free text, monitor values or an attached report) into a validated, machine-checkable specification: a baseline, a set of target values, and named pathophysiological modifiers, expressed *only* as commands drawn from a fixed allowlist and checked against the same parameter schema, unit conversions and physiological bounds as the interactive editor. The part that requires numerical rigour is handled by a **deterministic calibration layer**, which fits the model by assigning one physiologically interpretable lever to each measured target — the natural, monotone controller of that quantity — and driving each target to a clinician-meaningful tolerance with a robust one-dimensional root-finder, after allometric and gestational-age seeding and alignment of the baroreflex set-point so the model's own control loops defend rather than oppose the fit. The two roles are strictly separated: the language model performs no numerical fitting and never touches the model's equations or state, and the calibrator performs no interpretation. We show that for a representative 28-week, 1.0-kg preterm construction five targets converge within tolerance in two iterations while the untargeted vitals remain within preterm reference ranges, and that the same calibrator retunes an already-running simulation in place in three iterations; we also set out the conditions under which strongly coupled targets are, and are not, guaranteed to converge.

We believe this work fits the translational, mechanism-oriented scope of *Pediatric Research*. Its contribution is not a numerical technique for its own sake but the removal of the specific barrier that has kept interpretable physiological models from being individualized at the cot side: by turning an ill-posed high-dimensional fit into a set of well-posed one-dimensional root-finds chosen to respect the model's active regulation — a design we justify with a systematic sensitivity analysis — and by delegating only interpretation, never adjustment, to the language model, the method makes a patient-specific mechanistic newborn rapid, auditable and reproducible to build. This is the enabling step for using such models in neonatal education and physiological hypothesis-testing now, and, with prospective clinical validation, for individualized decision support. It is also, to our knowledge, the first method to couple a language model to a deterministic physiological calibrator in a way that is structurally prevented from letting the model invent or alter physiology — a safeguard we regard as essential to any clinical use of large language models and describe in full.

In the interest of full transparency, this manuscript is the **methodological companion of a planned series** describing the EXPLAIN model; the cardiovascular, respiratory, regulatory-organ, mechanical-support-device and integrated-model papers of the series describe the physiology this method parameterizes and are being submitted to *Pediatric Research* ‹state submitted/in-press/published status and cite once available›, each carrying a compact summary of this method and deferring its full treatment to the present paper. A companion paper applies the method to duct- and foramen-ovale-dependent congenital heart disease. This manuscript has been deposited as a preprint on bioRxiv (‹DOI›) so that the earlier papers may cite the method; we understand this does not constitute prior publication under the journal's policy, and we are happy to discuss the sequencing of the series with the editorial office. As the subject of this paper is itself a use of a large language model, we state plainly: a large language model (Claude, Anthropic, driven through the Claude Agent SDK) is used as the interpretation *component* of the method described; it interprets clinical inputs into validated, allowlisted specifications, performs no numerical fitting, does not modify the model's equations or state, and is not used to generate the scientific content or text of this study. No authorship is attributed to it; this is disclosed in the Methods.

The manuscript is original, has not been published previously (the bioRxiv preprint excepted, per the journal's policy), and is not under consideration elsewhere. All authors have approved the submission and meet ICMJE authorship criteria. We have no competing financial interests to declare ‹confirm›.
