## Block A — differentiated positioning (vs the incumbent in-silico neonatal models)

Two variants: a longer **Introduction** version (sets up the gap and motivates the paper) and a compact **Discussion** version (situates the results). Every primary paper (P1–P7) uses both; the Review (P8) expands the Intro version into its own section.

### A-Intro (≈190 words — for the Introduction)

> Mechanistic, closed-loop models of the neonatal circulation have an established place in this journal: an educational simulator of the haemodynamic transition at birth (Sá-Couto et al., 2010) and, more recently, closed-loop in-silico models of the fetal-to-neonatal transition built on time-varying-elastance or one-fibre myocardial mechanics with oxygen transport and homeostatic control (Munneke et al., 2021; van Willigen et al., 2026), alongside identifiable "digital-twin" formulations of the newborn circulation developed elsewhere (May et al., 2025). These models validate realistically against literature and animal data and are valuable for teaching and research, but they share two limits that bound their clinical reach. First, they are largely confined to the circulation with lumped gas exchange; they do not couple, in one model, a spontaneously breathing respiratory system, blood-gas and Stewart acid–base chemistry, the slower regulatory organs, and mechanical support. Second — and more fundamentally — they are typically run with generic rather than patient-specific parameters (the recent congenital-heart-disease transition model is evaluated explicitly "without patient-specific parameters"; van Willigen et al., 2026), because fitting a lumped-parameter model to an individual newborn has required slow, expert hand-tuning. EXPLAIN addresses both: it is a whole-body, real-time, openly available model (explain-modeling.com) whose patients are instantiated by an AI-assisted closed-loop calibration pipeline rather than by hand (Antonius et al., ‹P6›). ‹This paper examines ‹SUBSYSTEM/TOPIC›; the parameterization method it relies on is summarized in Box 1 and given in full in ‹P6›.›

### A-Discussion (≈110 words — for the Discussion)

> Our findings sit alongside a small but growing set of in-silico neonatal models in this journal (Sá-Couto et al., 2010; Munneke et al., 2021; van Willigen et al., 2026) and the identifiable newborn-circulation digital twins developed elsewhere (May et al., 2025). EXPLAIN differs from these in three respects that bear on the present results: it models the whole body rather than the circulation alone, so ‹SUBSYSTEM› interacts with the rest of the physiology as it would in vivo; its patients are parameterized automatically and reproducibly rather than by hand, so ‹the virtual patients used here› were generated by one audited pipeline; and it runs in real time in a browser, so the behaviour reported here is directly inspectable at explain-modeling.com. The limitation these models share with ours — validation against literature ranges rather than prospective individual-patient data — is considered below.

## Block B — compact AI-parameterization highlight (the series signature)

Every paper (P1–P8) carries this. Full **box** form is the default; a **short** form is provided for space-limited papers. Both defer the full method to P6 and both point to the Methods AI-disclosure. Fill the `‹targets ← levers›` slot from the table below.

### B-Box (default — a boxed callout, ≈180 words)

> **Box 1 · How the virtual patients in this paper were parameterized.**
> The patient profiles used here were not tuned by hand. EXPLAIN instantiates a patient with a two-layer, AI-assisted pipeline. An **interpretation layer** — a large language model — reads the available clinical description (free text, monitor values or a report) and emits a validated, bounded *specification*: a baseline, target values and named pathophysiology, expressed only through the same allowlisted, schema-checked commands as the interactive parameter editor. A **calibration layer** — a deterministic root-finder — then fits the model by assigning one physiologically interpretable lever to each target and driving that target to a clinician-meaningful tolerance with a proportional-seed/secant update, after allometric and gestational-age seeding and baroreflex set-point alignment so the model's own control loops defend rather than oppose the fit. The language model performs no numerical fitting and never edits equations or state. For this paper the relevant pairings are ‹TARGETS ← LEVERS›. The one-lever-per-target design is not assumed but **validated by a formal, variance-based sensitivity analysis** (Sobol′/PRCC, estimator-validated against an analytic benchmark), which also characterizes identifiability and the operating points where the design is weakest. The full method — convergence behaviour, this sensitivity-analysis validation, and the offline-construction and live-tuning entry points — is given in the companion paper (Antonius et al., ‹P6›); the use of a language model as a *method component*, not an author, is disclosed in the Methods.

### B-Short (2–3 sentences — where a box will not fit)

> **AI-parameterization (companion paper ‹P6›).** The virtual patients in this paper were not hand-tuned: a large language model interprets the clinical targets into a validated, allowlisted specification, and a deterministic calibrator fits the model by driving one physiologically interpretable lever per target to tolerance (with body-size and gestational-age seeding and baroreflex set-point alignment); the language model does no numerical fitting. Full method, convergence, and the variance-based sensitivity analysis validating the one-lever design (and flagging where it is weakest): Antonius et al., ‹P6›.
